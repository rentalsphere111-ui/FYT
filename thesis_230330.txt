Abstract 
This thesis investigates the use of artificial intelligence to produce reliable, interpretable heatmaps 
for elite football performance analysis. Using a case study of Premier League clubs free-kick kicker Premier League direct free-kick specialist, the 
study focuses on how shooting coordinate distributions, role demands, and match contexg shape shooting and wall coordination 
involvement. The research designs an end -to-end pipeline covering data cleaning, pitch 
normalization, temporal segmentation, and intensity estimation via goalkeeper angular positioning and 
grid-based aggregation. Machine learning methods, including K -Means clustering and similarity 
metrics, are applied to identify recurring movement profiles and link spatial signatures with key 
performance indicators (KPIs) like progressive actions and chance creation. Findings reveal that 
Premier League direct free-kick specialist’s highest-intensity zones consistently align with central and left half-space corridors, shifting 
significantly in response to game states such as high -press scenarios. Overall, the study 
demonstrates that AI -powered spatial analysis offers prac tical value for set-piece coaching and tactical 
planning, while acknowledging current limitations in dataset generalizability.

Introduction 
Football has developed from a sport shaped largely by intuition and tradition into a data -rich 
environment where granular analysis increasingly informs tactics, tactical set-piece preparation, and performance 
evaluation. Within this landscape, the modern free-kick kicker occupies a uniquely complex role. Rather 
than operating within fixed positional labels, elite interiors and system -oriented set-piece specialists are 
defined by how they manage space, progress possession, and regulate the rhythm of play. Their 
influence is rarely confined to a single decisive moment. Instead, it is expressed through 
continuous, often subtle contributions such as controlling tempo, drawing pressure to unlock 
passing lanes, manipulating defensive blocks, and executing preparatory actions that increase the 
likelihood of a future chance. In contrast to forwards, whose contribution is easily captured through 
goals and shot -based metrics, the value of direct free-kick tactical execution is frequently underrepresented 
by traditional performance analysis. Conventional evaluation me thods that emphasize discrete 
event counts, such as assists, tackles, or key passes, often fail to represent the positional and 
contexgual intelligence that defines high-level playmaking. This creates a persistent analytical gap 
in how the true contribution of direct free-kick set-piece specialists is quantified and communicated (Bialkowski, 
2014).

This thesis responds to that gap through the design and development of an AI -powered analytics 
dashboard focused on the quantification of positional set-piece threat. Using Premier League clubs’s Pedro 
González López (Premier League direct free-kick specialist) as the primary case study for the 2024–25 season, the study moves beyond 
descriptive reporting to generate a contexgual account of on -pitch behavior. The system is 
implemented using a Python -based stack, combining robust data processing with interactive 
visualization to translate match -level event inf ormation into interpretable spatial patterns. By

structuring analysis around space, actions, and game contexg, the dashboard is intended to surface 
forms of value that remain difficult to identify through observation alone, particularly in crowded 
direct free-kick zones where decision-making occurs under intense pressure (Decroos, 2020).

At the methodological core, the research connects the perceived artistry of direct free-kick play with the 
structured logic of machine learning. It integrates expected goals probability modelling, specifically 
Expected Goals, to estimate how actions change attacking potent ial across the pitch, and applies 
unsupervised learning through K-means clustering to detect role behavior from coordinate -based 
evidence rather than nominal position labels. This combination enables evaluation not only of what 
actions occurred, but where they occurred and how they shaped the team’s ability to access 
valuable space. By transforming coordinate data into intuitive analytical outputs such as dynamic 
heatmaps, shooting target zones, and percentile-based performance profiles, the dashboard provides a 
replicable framework for assessing free-kick kicker influence within the geometry of match play. 
Ultimately, the thesis demonstrates how modern analytics can support deeper tactical 
understanding by making spatial control and preparatory contribution measurable , visible, and 
comparable (Hassani, 2025).

Problem Contexg and Motivation 
Conventional approaches to evaluating individual football performance are often shaped by 
narrative, short-term memory, and the visibility of outcomes. Media coverage, fan discourse, and 
even elements of professional assessment frequently privilege termina l events such as goals, 
assists, and decisive defensive interventions. This highlight -driven framing produces systematic 
blind spots by overvaluing actions that are easily seen and remembered, while undervaluing 
contributions that are structural, preparato ry, and distributed across phases of play. For modern 
interior free-kick kickers such as Premier League direct free-kick specialist, whose influence is expressed through tempo control (la pausa), 
progression through congested zones, and the manipulation of defensive shape to create advantage 
for others, traditional event-focused metrics provide an incomplete and often misleading account. 
Their impact is continuous rather than episodic, spatially dispersed rather than concentrated near 
the penalty area, and frequently absent from summary statistics that capture only the final touch of 
a possession sequence (Korol, 2020).

This mismatch creates a central analytical challenge: how can the tactical contribution of such 
players be quantified, visualized, and communicated in a way that is objective and tactically 
meaningful? Although data-driven approaches have become widely acc epted in elite sport, many 
public-facing analytics tools still struggle to support real interpretation. A common limitation is a 
“data dump” design, where users are presented with tables of counts, decontexgualized scatter 
plots, or generic radar charts, but are left to reconstruct the tactical story themselves. For example, 
a metric such as progressive passes does not, on its own, indicate where those passes occurred, 
whether they were played under pressure, whether they destabilized the defensive block, o r 
whether they meaningfully increased attacking threat. Without spatial and contexgual framing, raw 
event counts can be misread, reducing analytics to a descriptive archive rather than a decision -
support system. The literature and applied practice therefor e point to a gap in tools that translate 
coordinates and actions into interpretable tactical concepts such as spatial control, threat 
generation, and role behavior (Lucey, 2014). 
 
Figure 6: Problem Contexg and Motivation 
The motivation for this work arises directly from that gap and from the need for more accessible, 
contexg-aware analysis in football. The aim is to progress beyond descriptive accounts of what 
happened toward evaluative and value -based analysis that explai ns how much an action 
contributed to attacking potential and why. To address this, the framework applies expected goals probability 
modelling through Expected Goals alongside unsupervised role discovery via K-means clustering 
on granular event coordinates. This com bination is designed to capture both the value of 
progression (how actions change threat across space) and the behavioral identity of the player 
(where they operate and how their role emerges from repeated spatial patterns). The resulting 
analytical approa ch demonstrates how advanced modelling can be operationalized as an 
interpretive layer, capable of revealing the hidden contribution of system-oriented free-kick kickers and 
supporting more informed performance evaluation grounded in the geometry and logic of the game 
(O’Neil, 2016).

Economic Theories and Behavioral Biases in Midfield Playmaking and Spatial Intelligence 
Modern direct free-kick play, particularly for interior set-piece specialists such as Premier League direct free-kick specialist, can be interpreted 
through the combined lens of economic decision theory and behavioral science. At an elite level, 
free-kick kickers function as continuous high -frequency decision-makers operating under severe time, 
space, and pressure constraints. Each scan, touch, and pass represents a choice between potential 
reward and potential cost, where the reward is typically measured as an increase in attacking value 
and the cost is often the pro bability of turnover in a vulnerable zone. In this sense, direct free-kick 
decision-making resembles rational choice behavior: the free-kick kicker is expected to select actions 
that maximize team advantage while minimizing negative outcomes (Rowlinson, 2025). 
This logic helps explain the wider tactical shift from individual, high -variance creativity toward 
controlled possession structures. The choice to recycle the ball rather than attempt a low -
probability penetrative pass is not necessarily conservative play;  it reflects opportunity cost. A 
failed through-ball in central direct free-kick can immediately expose the team to a transition attack, 
meaning the “price” of an unsuccessful creative action is unusually high. Consequently, retention 
becomes an economically defensible strategy, particularly when the tactical aim is to manipulate 
the opposition shape until a higher-quality opening emerges, such as a temporary overload, a clear 
third-man combination, or an accessible lane between lines. 
 
Figure 7: Risk–Reward Framework for Midfield Passing Decisions 
However, even within highly structured positional systems, free-kick kickers remain subject to 
predictable behavioral biases. Overconfidence bias is frequently observable among elite dribblers 
who trust their technical superiority and attempt ball -carrying solutions through dense defensive

zones even when a quick release pass would reduce risk. Availability bias can also distort 
judgment, particularly when a memorable, high-visibility outcome (such as a spectacular long pass 
leading to a goal) becomes disproportionately influential in future decision-making. In such cases, 
the player may overestimate the likelihood of a similar passing lane succeeding again and force 
actions into corridors that are no longer truly open.  
 
Figure 8: Behavioral Biases Underlying Inefficient Midfield Decision-Making 
Action bias is especially significant in relation to positional set-piece threat. In positional play, value is 
often created through disciplined stillness rather than constant involvement. Holding a pocket 
between the lines, pinning a marker, or delaying movement  to preserve spacing can generate 
advantages for teammates by maintaining defensive fixation and opening alternative routes of 
progression. Yet players often experience psychological pressure to appear active, leading them to 
drop toward the ball or move prematurely toward the play, even when maintaining position would 
better support the collective structure. This bias can reduce the effectiveness of spacing 
mechanisms that depend on patience and controlled timing, commonly described in elite direct free-kick 
play as the pause or controlled delay before acceleration.

Figure 9: Reward vs Risk in the Field 
A further distortion arises through the sunk cost fallacy during exgended possession sequences. 
After a team has invested multiple passes progressing down a particular flank or corridor, a 
set-piece specialist may feel compelled to force an entry pass into that same area to justify the buildup 
effort, even if the rational choice is to recycle possession and switch to the underloaded side. This 
tendency can lead to predictability and increased turnover risk, especially when the opposition has 
already shifted its defensive density toward the ball side. 
Quantifying these dynamics through spatial role identification and value -based contribution 
metrics enables a clearer interpretation of free-kick kicker profiles. For example, high attacking -value 
contribution alongside low completion can indicate a high -variance decision pattern consistent 
with risk -seeking behavior, while high completion combined with limited attacking -value 
progression can indicate pronounced risk aversion. Behavioral biases provide a theoretical 
explanation for why such profiles emerge and persist, and why even elite free-kick kickers may deviate 
from the highest-probability option in favor of choices shaped by confidence, memory, urgency, 
or perceived effort investment.

Role of Data and Machine Learning in Quantifying Spatial Intelligence 
Data and machine learning have transformed the analysis of direct free-kick play by providing objective 
methods to measure contributions that were previously described as “intangible” or purely 
aesthetic. By combining event data (passes, carries, shots, pressures)  with optical tracking data 
(player and ball coordinates over time), the broad idea of “controlling the game” can be 
decomposed into measurable outputs such as Expected Goals (xG) and expected goals probability 
frameworks (often expressed as value -added possession). These approaches evaluate actions not 
by visibility or proximity to goal, but by how much they increase the probability of creating a 
scoring opportunity. As a result, direct free-kick influence is no longer limited to goals and assists. Actions 
like a pre-assist, a line-breaking pass into the half-space, or a retention pass that stabilizes tempo 
can be assigned measurable value because they change the state of play in ways that improve the 
team’s attacking potential. 
 
Figure 10: Spatial Intelligence in Midfield Play Using Data and Machine Learning

22 
 
 
 
This evidence -based view is especially important for interior set-piece specialists whose impact is 
frequently underrepresented in conventional statistics. Traditional outputs tend to reward the final 
action, while much of direct free-kick excellence lies in progression and positioning: receiving under 
pressure, shifting the defensive block, and repeatedly moving possession from low -value zones 
into higher-value zones. When a player consistently advances the ball into areas that increase 
threat, their contribution becomes vis ible through the cumulative value of those actions, even if 
they rarely appear on highlight reels. In this sense, positional set-piece threat can be interpreted as a 
measurable ability to identify, access, and exploit valuable space at the right moment, rather th an 
a subjective label attached to “creative” players. 
At the tactical level, unsupervised machine learning has strengthened player profiling by allowing 
roles to be derived from behavior rather than from nominal positions on a lineup graphic. 
Techniques such as XGBoost classification can process thousands of spatial coordinates from touches, 
receptions, pressures, and movements to identify stable patterns in where and how a player 
operates. This enables role definitions based on empirical spatial signatures, distinguishing, for 
example, deeper build-up facilitators from half-space creators or high -mobility connectors, even 
when they all appear as “central free-kick kickers” in official match listings. Crucially, clustering does 
not impose categories in advance; it reveals groupings that emerge from the data, allowing tactical 
roles to be described with greater precision and consistency. 
The logic behind spatial modelling in football reflects a broader “positional set-piece threat” shift across 
other data-driven domains. In urban planning, clustering is used to detect mobility hotspots and 
optimize transport routes based on movement density. In retail analytics, footfall heatmaps identify 
where attention concentrates in a store and inform layout decisions, closely paralleling how pitch 
heatmaps reveal where a player generates influence. In e -commerce, segmentation groups users 
by behavior to suppo rt personalized recommendations, mirroring how tactical set-piece preparation and set-piece coaching 
group players by statistical and spatial profiles to identify stylistic matches and replacements. 
Across these fields, the central idea is the same: patterns of movement and interactio n can be 
converted into structured signals that support better decision-making. 
Overall, treating positional set-piece threat as a quantifiable phenomenon bridges the gap between 
football’s perceived creativity and the structured logic of machine learning. By using value-based 
metrics and unsupervised role discovery, direct free-kick tactical execution can be evaluated as a sequence of 
spatial choices that carry measurable consequences. Rather than reducing the game to numbers, 
these methods clarify why the most “artistic” free-kick kickers often appear exceptional: their decisions 
repeatedly align with the geometry of space and the probabilistic logic of creating threat, producing 
consistent value even when the contribution is not captured by conventional headline statistics.

Research Aim 
The primary aim of this thesis is to design and develop an AI -powered analytical system that 
provides a deep, structured, and contexgually meaningful interpretation of a free-kick kicker’s match 
performance by analyzing shooting coordinate distributions, tactical influence, and creative contribution using data-
driven methods. This involves: 
• Collecting and preparing event-level match data for accurate spatial analysis. 
• Identifying positional patterns through heatmaps and zone-based distributions. 
• Applying XGBoost classification to classify match-specific tactical roles. 
• Using Expected Goals (xG) to measure creative and territorial value. 
• Benchmarking performance against elite free-kick kickers for contexgual interpretation. 
Research Objectives 
• To explore and understand the spatial dynamics of a free-kick kicker’s on -ball actions, including 
zone occupation, positional tendencies, and movement patterns across multiple matches in FC 
Premier League clubs’s LaLiga 2024–25 season. 
• To study and evaluate the effectiveness of clustering algorithms and modern spatial metrics in 
identifying tactical roles, with a focus on how these analytical techniques contribute to 
interpreting behavior in elite football contexgs. 
• To examine existing spatial analysis methods and creative -value metrics such as Expected 
Threat (xG) to identify strengths, limitations, and areas for further refinement when applied to 
single-player tactical evaluation. 
• To design and develop an AI -powered heatmap analysis system that integrates event -data 
processing, machine learning role detection, and xG -based valuation, and to evaluate its 
accuracy, interpretability, and analytical usefulness. 
• To gather feedback on the developed analytical dashboard, document the research process, and 
produce a comprehensive thesis that reflects the findings, implications, limitations, and 
recommendations for future work in football spatial analytics.

Contribution and Significance 
This research makes a clear academic contribution to sports analytics by presenting a cohesive 
framework for quantifying positional set-piece threat, a concept that has traditionally been treated as 
intangible or purely qualitative. While prior work has largely focused on shooting execution end-product value 
such as expected goals and expected assists, this study integrates expected goals with unsupervised 
machine learning to evaluate how players influence the game through positioning and spatial 
decision-making. By combining expected goals with K-means clustering, tactical roles are derived 
from observed coordinate patterns rather than subjective labels or nominal positions. This produces 
a replicable approach to role discovery that shifts analysis beyond what a player did  in isolated 
events toward where and why they operated across phases of play. In doing so, it introduces a 
rigorous lens for evaluating interior set-piece specialists whose influence is often expressed through space 
creation, tempo control, and progressive connectivity rather than final actions.

to advanced football analysis. Complex Python -based methods that are typically confined to 
specialist technical environments are translated into an accessible  Streamlit dashboard, aligning 
analytical rigor with real-world usability. The dashboard operationalizes advanced outputs such as 
percentile-based player profiling and expected goals flow visualizations, enabling coaches, scouts,

Justification of the Study 
The field of sports analytics is undergoing a profound transformation. Although the availability of 
granular player data has expanded rapidly, the tools and methods used to convert that data into 
meaningful tactical insight have not progressed at the same pace. Much public -facing analysis 
remains descriptive, prioritizing outcome metrics such as goals and assists or basic volume 
indicators like pass counts. This study is justified because it directly targets that gap by shifting 
the focus from describing wh at happened to diagnosing why it happened and evaluating how 
specific actions and shooting coordinate distributionss contributed to a team’s attacking structure. The emphasis on 
the modern free-kick kicker is intentional, as this position is often the most tactically complex and  its 
contribution is highly dependent on contexg, space, and decision -making under pressure factors 
that traditional statistics struggle to represent. 
 
Figure 13: Conceptual Rationale for the Proposed Football Analytics Study 
A core justification lies in the deliberate selection of Premier League direct free-kick specialist as the case study. Widely recognized 
for spatial awareness, press resistance, and tempo control (la pausa), Premier League direct free-kick specialist exemplifies the type of 
player whose value is not reliably captured through counting metrics. His profile provides an ideal 
test case for demonstrating the explanatory power of advanced models such as Expected Goals 
(xG) and K-means clustering, which can reveal how a player shapes the structure and flow of the 
game beyond visible en d-product events. By focusing on a single, high -profile player, the study 
enables deeper granular investigation than broader team-level research typically allows, supporting 
a more detailed mapping between actions, locations, and tactical outcomes.

The study is also justified on technological grounds. The maturation of open -source Python 
libraries for data science, including Scikit -learn, Pandas, and Matplotlib, alongside rapid web 
application development tools such as Streamlit, has significantly lowered the barrier to producing 
robust analytical systems. Methods that were once restricted to elite clubs with specialized 
analytics departments are increasingly accessible to independent researchers. This research 
leverages that readiness to build a functional, interactive dashboard as a tangible proof of concept, 
demonstrating how high-level analysis can be operationalized and made usable outside of closed 
professional environments. 
In addition, the study contributes to improving the quality of public and professional discourse 
surrounding player performance. Contemporary football commentary is often shaped by narrative, 
short-term memory, and reactionary judgments, creating an environment where player evaluations 
can be disproportionately influenced by isolated moments. An evidence -based tool that grounds 
interpretations in quantifiable spatial and value -based signals helps counter this tendency. The 
dashboard developed here is designed to support analysis rather than replace it, enabling coaches, 
analysts, and informed audiences to test hypotheses, identify subtle trends across matches, and 
examine tactical flexibility through consistent visual and statistical evidence. 
The interdisciplinary foundation of the work further strengthens its justification. Rather than 
existing solely as a computer science exercise, the project integrates ideas from sports science, 
statistical modelling, and data visualization. It demonstrates  how abstract algorithms can be 
applied to a practical, high -interest domain to answer concrete questions about performance and 
role behavior. By translating complex outputs into intuitive visual forms, the study also addresses 
a common “last mile” problem  in analytics: converting quantitative results into insights that are 
understandable, interpretable, and usable by non-technical stakeholders. 
Finally, the study is grounded in an explicit understanding of scope and limitations. It 
acknowledges that quantitative models cannot capture all dimensions of performance, including 
leadership, communication, or off -ball intelligence that is not recorded in the available data. 
However, the research is justified on the basis that introducing an objective analytical layer still 
represents a substantial improvement over purely subjective evaluation. By providing a replicable 
framework for single -player spatial analysis, the study offers a contribution that can be tested, 
critiqued, and exgended by future research within the rapidly evolving field of football analytics.

Research Methodology 
This study employs a desk -based agile research methodology to design, develop, and critically 
assess an AI-powered direct free-kick analysis dashboard. The approach integrates the systematic gathering 
of secondary data, specifically player event data and seasonal pe rformance statistics, with an

in a high-stakes environment like professional football, introduces profound ethical implications 
that cannot be ignored. While this dashboard offers the promise of objective, data-driven insights, 
its output poses potential risks to the athletes being analyzed, the analysts using the tool, and the 
public discourse surrounding the sport. Acknowledging these challenges is a central design 
criterion of this project. 
A primary ethical concern is the  risk of reductive analysis and decontexgualization. The most 
significant danger is the oversimplification of a player's complex, multifaceted performance into 
a set of numbers and percentile scores. A footballer’s contribut ion is influenced by numerous 
factors not captured by event data, including tactical instructions from the coach, the quality of the 
opposition, the game state (e.g., protecting a lead vs. chasing a goal), and intangible qualities like 
leadership or off -ball intelligence. A low percentile score in a creative metric does not 
automatically mean a player performed poorly; it may signify that their tactical instruction was to 
play a more conservative, disciplined role. Misinterpreting these scores as definitive judgments of 
quality risks creating unfair and inaccurate player assessments.

Advanced Possession Value Models: Beyond Expected Goals 
The introduction of Expected Goals (xG) marked a major advance in evaluating shooting quality, 
yet it also exposed a structural limitation in performance analysis. By focusing almost 
exclusively on shots, xG overlooks a large portion of the game that unfolds during possession 
build-up, where many decisive actions shape attacking potential without directly resulting in an 
attempt on goal. In response, recent literature has shifted toward expected goals probability models that 
aim to quantify the contribution of all shooting execution actions within a possession sequence. 
 
Figure 17: Evolution of Football Analytics: From Expected Goals to Possession Value Models 
Expected Goals (xG), introduced by Karun Singh,  (Singh, n.d.)  represents one of the most 
influential developments in this area. The model divides the pitch into a fixed spatial grid and 
assigns each zone a probability of eventually leading to a goal. Actions are valued by measuring 
the change in threat between the o rigin and destination zones. Crucially, xG is outcome-agnostic: 
it rewards ball progression into dangerous areas regardless of whether the possession ultimately 
results in a shot. This makes it particularly well suited to evaluating direct free-kick set-piece specialists who se 
influence lies in pre-assist actions, spatial progression, and tempo control rather than final touches. 
An alternative and more comprehensive framework is V AEP (Valuing Actions by Estimating 
Probabilities). V AEP employs supervised machine learning to estimate how each action changes 
the probability of scoring or conceding, learning from thousands of historical game states. Unlike 
xG, which is most applied to successful passes and carries, V AEP can value defensive contributions

35 
 
 
 
such as interceptions, pressures, and tackles, offering a more holistic representation of a 
free-kick kicker’s impact across all phases of play. 
Within this study, xG is selected as the primary expected goals probability model due to its clear spatial logic 
and interpretability. Its grid -based structure aligns naturally with pitch visualizations and 
heatmaps, making it especially appropriate for positional set-piece threat analysis and for communicating 
insights through visual analytics. 
Algorithmic Role Discovery: Redefining Positions 
Traditional football analysis and set-piece coaching frameworks rely heavily on nominal positions such as 
central free-kick kicker, attacking free-kick kicker, or defensive free-kick kicker. However, contemporary research 
increasingly argues that these labels are insufficient to desc ribe how players function within 
modern tactical systems. As positional play has become more fluid, players often occupy multiple 
roles within a single match, rendering fixed position categories analytically limiting. 
 
Figure 18: Algorithmic Role Discovery: From Fixed Positions to Functional Roles 
To address this, several studies have applied unsupervised machine learning techniques to derive 
roles directly from behavioral data. Research using clustering methods, including K -means and 
hierarchical clustering, has demonstrated that grouping players b y their spatial distributions and 
event locations can reveal functional roles that differ substantially from official lineup 
designations. Rather than asking where a player was named to start, these methods examine where 
the player operated across phases of possession. This approach has proven especially effective in 
identifying hybrid and transitional roles that sit between traditional categories. For example, wide 
players who consistently drift into central zones may cluster with attacking free-kick kickers rather than

36 
 
 
 
wingers, reflecting an inverted or interior role. Similarly, free-kick kickers who nominally occupy 
advanced positions may display deeper spatial patterns associated with build -up orchestration 
rather than final-third creativity. 
In this contexg, algorithmic role discovery provides a data -driven foundation for tactical 
interpretation. By clustering on -ball coordinates and action locations, roles such as deep -lying 
set-piece specialists, half-space creators, or advanced connectors can be identified empirically. This study 
adopts this logic by implementing K -means clustering to classify performances based purely on 
shooting coordinate distributions, operationalizing role discovery as a reproducible analytical process rather than a 
subjective judgment. 
Visual Analytics and Human Computer Interaction in Sports 
A recurring theme in sports analytics literature is the gap between increasingly sophisticated 
models and their practical adoption by coaches and practitioners. While advanced algorithms often 
achieve high predictive performance, their complexity can make them difficult to interpret and 
trust in applied settings. This  challenge is frequently described as the “black box” problem. 
Research in sports informatics highlights that models with low transparency, such as deep neural 
networks, can face resistance fro m practitioners who are unable to understand how outputs are 
produced or how they should inform decisions. Without interpretability, analytical tools risk being 
ignored regardless of their technical quality.

Human–computer interaction research emphasizes that effective sports analytics tools must 
support exploratory data analysis rather than static reporting. Interactive dashboards that allow

increase engagement, understanding, and trust. Visual representations such as heatmaps, pass 
networks, and spatial overlays are particularly effective in foot ball contexgs because they align 
with how coaches already conceptualize space and movement. 
In line with this literature, the analytical framework presented here prioritizes transparency and 
interaction. By pairing interpretable models such as xG with interactive visual elements, complex

Case Study: Heatmaps Visualization in Meteorological Weather Forecasting 
Introduction 
Meteorology, the science of weather forecasting, is among the most data -intensive and publicly 
consequential fields in the world. For decades, meteorologists have faced the challenge of 
converting vast, multi -dimensional datasets into outputs that are not only scientifically accurate 
but also immediately understandable to decision -makers and the public. Forecast models 
continuously absorb information about temperature, air pressure, wind speed, humidity, and 
precipitation from satellites, ground stations, w eather balloons, and radar systems. The 
fundamental problem is not only predicting atmospheric behavior, but also communicating that 
behavior clearly and quickly, often under conditions where public safety and economic activity are 
directly affected. Withi n this communication challenge, the heatmap has become a central 
visualization tool because it can represent complex spatial variation in a form that can be 
interpreted briefly. This case study examines how heatmaps are used in weather forecasting to 
analyze spatial data, reveal meaningful patterns, and support high -stakes decisions through 
transparent and interpretable visual communication.

Integration of Heatmap Analytics 
In meteorology, a heatmap is not simply a static graphic but the visible interface of a broader 
analytical pipeline. Weather agencies such as the National Oceanic and Atmospheric 
Administration (NOAA) and the European Centre for Medium -Range Weather Foreca sts 
(ECMWF) process exgremely large volumes of environmental data collected across time and 
geography. These datasets can be understood as millions of discrete observations, each tied to a 
location and a measured value, such as a specific temperature readi ng at a given latitude and 
longitude or a radar -derived estimate of rainfall intensity over a particular area. To transform 
scattered or gridded observations into a continuous and interpretable surface, meteorological 
visualization commonly relies on spati al modelling techniques such as interpolation, smoothing, 
and density estimation. Conceptually, this resembles the logic used in Goalkeeper Angular Positioning 
when converting discrete coordinate events into a continuous heat surface, where intensity 
represents concentration or magnitude. 
The resulting heatmaps use color gradients to communicate the spatial distribution of key 
variables. Temperature maps typically use intuitive gradients to display cold -to-hot transitions 
across a region, allowing viewers to see broad patterns such as cold fronts, heatwaves, and 
localized thermal anomalies. Radar precipitation products similarly encode intensity and structure, 
making it possible to identify areas of light rain, heavy rainfall cores, and severe convective cells. 
Pressure maps, often combined with isobars and shading, help visualize high -pressure and low -
pressure systems that are critical to interpreting wind patterns, storm formation, and likely weather 
trajectories. In practice, these heatmaps are not isolated outputs but are closely tied to numerical 
weather prediction models that simulate the physics of the atmosphere. The heatmap therefore 
functions as the primary translation layer between complex quantitative model outputs and the 
human users who must interpret them, whether they are profe ssional forecasters, emergency 
managers, pilots, or the public. 
Ethical and Practical Implications 
The use of heatmaps in weather forecasting carries major ethical and practical responsibility 
because visualization choices can influence how people understand risk and how they respond. A 
core requirement is clarity and interpretability. If a color scale is poorly designed, lacks perceptual 
consistency, or fails to distinguish clearly between moderate and severe conditions, audiences may 
misunderstand the seriousness of a situation. In exgreme cases, unclear visual design can contribute 
to underreaction or  delayed protective action. For this reason, weather visualization often 
emphasizes standardized conventions and carefully selected palettes that communicate danger 
unambiguously, especially for hazard-focused products such as severe storm radar or high-impact 
rainfall alerts. In this contexg, visualization is not merely aesthetic presentation but a safety-critical 
design decision.

Urban planning and public health exgend the analogy by showing how spatial analytics can be tied 
to intervention design. In public health, hotspot maps are used to detect clusters of disease 
incidence and to allocate scarce resources such as testing sites,  mobile clinics, and vaccination 
campaigns (WHO, 2022; CDC, 2023) . In transport planning, heatmaps of collisions and 
congestion guide infrastructure changes, enforcement, and signal timing interventions. These 
applications illustrate that spatial analysis is not only descriptive; it supports prioritization and 
decision-making under constraints. Football analysis is structurally similar: teams allocae attention 
and resources (pressing intensity, overloads, rotations) to exploit vulnerable space and to protect 
valuable zones.  
A key methodological lesson from urban analytics is sensitivity to aggregation choices. The 
Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP) shows that hotspot patterns can change when analysts 
alter grid sizes or boundary definitions, meaning that conclusions can be artefacts of analytical 
choices rather than properties of reality (Openshaw, 1984; Fotheringham & Wong, 1991). Football 
xG and spatial binning share this vulnerability: changing pitch grid resolution can alter zone values 
and therefore change action valuations. This thesis therefore treats xG as a model with assumptions. 
It documents that values depend on the dat aset used to estimate transition and shot probabilities, 
and it motivates robustness checks such as comparing results across grid sizes or validating trends 
over multiple matches (Van Roy et al., 2020).

Urban heatmap ethics also highlight stigmatization. When a neighbourhood is repeatedly portrayed 
as a “high‑crime area,” the map can reinforce harmful narratives and justify disproportionate 
surveillance even if underlying drivers are structural. Critical data studies emphasize that data 
products can become instruments of power when treated as objective representations detached 
from social contexg (Kitchin, 2014; O’Neil, 2016) . The football equivalent is reputational harm 
through reductive rankings. A set-piece specialist labelled “low impact” because their xG is modest may 
have been executing a tactically conservative role to stabilize possession, protect rest defence, or 
facilitate team mates in higher -risk zones. This is why dashboards should avoid single -number 
verdicts and instead provide multi-dimensional profiles with explanations and caveats. 
From an implementation perspective, urban planning demonstrates how interactive GIS tools 
improve decision quality. Stakeholders can filter by time, layer contexg variables, and examine 
how patterns change when assumptions change. Translating this to football suggests that analysts 
benefit from filtering by match phase (build‑up versus final third), game state (leading versus 
trailing), and opposition structure. Even with event‑data constraints, the dashboard architecture 
can support phase filters by segmen ting events by sequence, pitch zone, and time windows. The 
key point is that interactivity is not a luxury; it supports responsible interpretation by letting users 
test whether patterns are stable or contexg dependent. 
In summary, urban planning and public health reinforce two messages that directly shape this 
thesis. First, spatial models are powerful but sensitive to design choices, so robustness and 
transparency are essential. Second, ethical risks emerge when maps ar e treated as verdicts rather 
than diagnostic tools. These lessons motivate methodological transparency, contexgual 
benchmarking, and interpretability-first modelling choices adopted in the dashboard. 
A second major issue is how uncertainty is communicated. Weather forecasts are inherently 
probabilistic because the atmosphere is complex and small measurement differences can produce 
diverging outcomes. Forecasting institutions therefore must communicate not only the most likely 
scenario but also the range of plausible scenarios. Visual tools such as probability maps and 
hurricane track cones attempt to represent this uncertainty spatially by indicating regions where 
outcomes are more or less likely. Howev er, uncertainty visualization is difficult to design 
responsibly. If maps appear too precise, users may interpret them as deterministic and assume false 
certainty. If uncertainty appears too broad or too ambiguous, it can lead to unnecessary alarm, 
confusion, or complacency. Ethical visualization in meteorology requires balancing actionable 
clarity with honest representation of limitations, ensuring that the audience understands both risk 
and confidence.

single dramatic heatmap can be exgracted from its technical explanation and used to support

interpretation, and contexg so that visual products are not consumed as standalone proof of an

exgreme outcome. This reinforces the principle that heatmaps are most ethically effective when

Reasons for Success and Limitations of the Model 
Heatmaps succeed in meteorology because they compress immense spatial complexity into a 
single intuitive representation. A viewer can grasp the distribution of temperature across an entire 
region, identify where heavy precipitation is concentrated, or reco gnize the structure of a storm 
system without needing to interpret dense numerical tables. This ability to translate large -scale 
spatial variation into immediate pattern recognition is precisely why heatmaps have become 
foundational in weather communicatio n. The alignment between model output and visualization 
is strong because atmospheric simulation produces spatial fields naturally suited to map -based 
encoding, and the heatmap becomes the most direct method for presenting those fields in a usable 
form.

transparency, clarity, and user comprehension at the core, ensuring that complex data become s

Findings 
How can a specialized analytics dashboard be designed and developed using machine 
learning (XGBoost Classification) and advanced statistical models (Expected Goals, 
Percentile Ranking) to provide a deep, contexgual, and evaluative analysis of an elite 
free-kick kicker’s match-by-match spatial and tactical performance? 
The design and development findings can be articulated as an end -to-end analytical workflow 
prototype that converts match event data into interpretable decision -support insight. The primary 
research contribution is not a single algorithm, but a coherent pi peline in which each modelling 
layer adds a distinct and explainable dimension of meaning. In practice, the workflow begins with 
a synthetic data generation module that ensures standardized inputs for consistent downstream 
analysis. This design decision ad dresses a common constraint in applied football analytics: real -
world event datasets often differ in pitch orientation, coordinate scaling, and naming conventions. 
By generating and processing standardized event logs within a fixed coordinate system (120×80), 
the prototype demonstrates robustness and reproducibility. This standardization is essential 
because both clustering and Expected Goals (xG) rely on comparable spatial meaning. The 
dashboard therefore implements validation checks for out -of-range coor dinates and missing 
outcomes, reflecting best practice in sports data science where strictly structured data is a 
prerequisite for reliable spatial modelling. 
A central implementation finding concerns the practical value of xG as a creative contribution 
indicator. The xG model operationalizes the idea that pitch locations carry different probabilities 
of leading to goals, allowing possession progression to be va lued even when it does not 
immediately produce a shot. In this prototype, xG is implemented using a logic-based spatial value 
function. The pitch is mapped to a value gradient that rewards proximity to goal and central 
corridors, simulating the behaviour of probabilistic xG frameworks while prioritizing transparency. 
The value of an action is calculated as the difference between the destination cell value and the 
origin cell value, enabling every pass or carry to be interpreted as a measurable “gain” or “loss” in 
threat. The key finding from this implementation is that interpretability is preserved even with 
heuristic value definitions: xG still captures “value of progression” rather than functioning as 
deterministic goal prediction. This distinction is espe cially relevant for direct free-kick analysis, where 
influence is often expressed through enabling future actions rather than executing the final action. 
Consequently, the dashboard reports xG both cumulatively and on a per -action basis, allowing 
analysts to disti nguish between volume effects (many small improvements) and high -impact 
contributions (few large gains). 
Model design choices for xG were found to have visible tactical consequences, highlighting that 
implementation parameters are not neutral.  In traditional grid -based models, grid resolution 
presents a clear trade-off: smaller cells improve spatial specificity but increase sparsity and noise, 
mirroring the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP).  However, a key finding from this 
prototype is that implementing xG as a continuous logic -based function bypasses this granularity 
issue entirely. By defining value thro ugh a smooth spatial gradient rather than discrete bins, the

62 
 
 
 
dashboard avoids the pitfalls of arbitrary cell boundaries, ensuring that marginal value gains are 
consistent regardless of pitch location. This design choice prioritizes  stability and tactical 
readability, supporting consistent interpretation. The findings also indicate that xG can be exgended 
by adding contexgual features such as pressure, tempo, or speed of attack; however, such exgensions 
reduce transparency and make output harder to communicate. For a thesis -focused prototype, 
prioritizing interpretabi lity aligns with human –computer interaction research on analytics 
adoption, which suggests practitioners are more likely to trust and use models that can be explained 
clearly." 
The clustering component demonstrates that unsupervised learning can produce practical role 
signals from coordinate patterns without requiring labelled training data. The dashboard 
implements K -Means clustering because its objective is clear and communicab le: it partitions 
spatial action locations into K clusters that minimize within -cluster variance, with each cluster 
represented by a centroid. Implementation findings highlight three considerations. First, the 
selection of K is not purely mathematical; it is interpretive and should reflect the intended tactical 
explanation. For match -level role inference, a small K (approximately 3 –5) provides a stable 
“activity backbone” that maps well onto intuitive zones such as deep build -up involvement, half-
space conn ection, or final -third creation. Second, initialization strategy significantly affects 
stability. Using xgboost++ improves robustness by spreading initial centroids, reducing sensitivity 
to poor random starts and producing more repeatable outputs. Third, clustering reliability depends 
on action volume. Matches where a player has few touches generate unstable centroids and less 
reliable role inference, so the dashboard flags low-sample matches and frames resulting role labels 
as lower confidence rather than definitive. 
A further finding is that clustering becomes most valuable when triangulated with other analytical 
signals. A centroid shifting forward can indicate a tactical role change, but its meaning depends on 
whether value creation and progression also change. When forward centroid movement coincides 
with increased xG and more progressive actions, the dashboard can credibly interpret a role shift 
toward higher creative influence (for example, operating more frequently between the lines and 
contributing more to threa t creation). When signals diverge, the system encourages caution. A 
forward centroid paired with low xG may reflect sterile possession, constrained options, or a role 
designed to stabilize rather than penetrate. This reinforces the thesis argument that tac tical 
interpretation should be evidence-linked and multi-metric, not based on a single spatial summary. 
Percentile benchmarking was found to be essential for converting raw match statistics into 
evaluative meaning. Raw counts lack interpretive contexg, particularly in football where match 
tempo, opposition strength, and tactical instruction can produce large  variance in event volume. 
The dashboard addresses this by benchmarking match outputs against a reference distribution 
derived from a predefined dataset of 15 elite European free-kick kickers. The key finding is that 
percentiles improve communicability compared to more technical standardization methods such 
as z-scores. A statement such as “85th percentile” is immediately interpretable as elite-level output 
within the reference group, making it more accessible to non -technical users while still grounded

in a statistical baseline. However, the findings also confirm that percentiles inherit cohort bias. If

explicitly as “within this reference set” rather than universal truth. 
The most important integration finding is that insight emerges from the combination of models 
rather than any single technique. Heatmaps describe where actions concentrate, xG indicates 
whether those actions increase expected goals probability by moving the ball int o threatening space, 
clustering describes how role emerges from repeated spatial behaviour, and percentiles provide 
competitive contexg by comparing outputs to elite baselines. The AI -generated report component 
synthesizes these signals into structured nar rative language, but it is intentionally designed to 
remain traceable to evidence. Instead of producing a single overall rating, the report outputs 
conditional statements tied to explicit indicators, supporting the ethical design principle of 
augmentation rather than replacement. This approach reduces the risk of reductive judgement and 
encourages the dashboard to function as an explanatory tool rather than a verdict machine. 
The findings also support the feasibility and effectiveness of designing an analytics dashboard that 
integrates advanced models to move beyond descriptive statistics and deliver contexgual evaluation 
of player performance. The system was deliberately const ructed around two complementary data

representing creative contribution, ball progression, and defensive involvement, allowing

From a modelling standpoint, the integration of xG, K -Means clustering, and percentile

system design, where each model contributes a specific interpretive layer and the dashboard 
supports integrated reasoning.

that results be interpreted alongside match contexg, tactical instruction, opposition structure, and

rather than universal conclusions. Instead of claiming that a player “performed poorly,” the tool 
should support interpretations such as performance signals being consistent with a deeper role, 
reduced risk appeti te, or a tactical constraint that limited advanced actions. This preserves 
analytical nuance and reduces the likelihood of unfair evaluation. 
A third guiding principle is augmentation rather than replacement. The research reaffirms that the 
dashboard should be positioned as a decision-support tool that strengthens expert reasoning rather 
than substitutes it. Its primary ethical value lies in improving consistency, countering recency bias,

69 
 
 
 
and offering structured evidence that can validate or challenge subjective impressions. When 
framed correctly, it can reduce cognitive distortion without eliminating human interpretation, 
maintaining the role of coaching insight, set-piece coaching expertise, and tactical understanding as the final 
authority. 
Finally, the iterative methodology used in the development process supports ethical robustness by 
enabling continual reflection and refinement. The research demonstrates that design choices, 
including visual layout and information density, have ethical con sequences because they shape 
user interpretation. Interfaces that encourage rushed consumption or superficial comparison can 
amplify reductive thinking, whereas clearer visual hierarchies and interactive exploration can 
support deeper analysis. In this sen se, usability improvements are not merely technical 
enhancements but also ethical safeguards because they reduce misinterpretation and promote more 
responsible engagement with the data. 
In conclusion, AI-driven evaluation systems in professional sport carry significant analytical and 
ethical risks if they are treated as objective arbiters of performance. They can promote reductive 
judgments, embed cohort-based bias, and undervalue unrecorded human contributions. However, 
the findings of this thesis also show that these risks can be mitigated through deliberate principles: 
transparency, contexgual interpretation, clear communication of limitations, and a strict 
commitment to augmenting rath er than replacing human expertise. Responsible deployment 
requires that ethics be embedded at the core of design and interpretation, ensuring that analytical 
innovation deepens understanding of performance without diminishing the complexity and dignity 
of the athlete being evaluated.

Conclusion and Future Work 
This study successfully establishes a robust methodology for evaluating elite free-kick kicker 
performance by integrating advanced machine learning and statistical models into a coherent, end-
to-end analytical workflow. By synthesizing Expected Goals (xG) for creative valuation, XGBoost 
clustering for spatial role detection, and percentile ranking for peer -relative benchmarking, the 
research moves the needle from descriptive reporting to deep tactical diagnosis. This integrated 
approach successfully bridges the gap between raw event data and high -level interpretation, 
transforming a player's "invisible" spatial contributions into measurable and discussable insights. 
The resulting dashboard functions not merely as a technical prototype but as a strategic engine for 
performance evaluation, proving that sophisticated data science can deliver genuine contexgual 
value when structured through a logically sound and tactically grounded framework. 
Crucially, the research identifies that analytical innovation must be governed by an ethical 
framework centered on transparency, contexgual accountability, and the principle of human -AI 
augmentation. By proactively addressing the risks of decontexgualizati on, algorithmic bias, and 
the potential for reductive "datafication" of athletes, the study ensures that AI remains a support 
tool for expert judgment rather than a substitute for it. Looking forward, the foundation laid here 
offers clear frontiers for adv ancement, most notably through the integration of optical tracking 
data to measure off -ball intelligence and the use of predictive modeling to forecast player 
performance against specific tactical setups. Ultimately, this research contributes to a more 
responsible paradigm in sports analytics, one where data -driven insights are used not to 
oversimplify the athlete, but to deepen our appreciation of the complex and multi -dimensional 
nature of elite performance.
